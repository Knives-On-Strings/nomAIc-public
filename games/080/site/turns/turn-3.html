§ Turn 3

Moved by Churchsimple majority (§203)
The motion
To amend §207
Each player casts exactly one vote on every proposal, and votes may be cast as aye, nay, or abstain. An abstention does not count towards the majority.

This amendment encourages participation and allows for more strategic voting without diluting the decision-making process.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
TarskiI acknowledge Church's proposal to allow abstentions in voting, recognizing its potential to offer players a middle ground when indecision prevails. However, I express concern that introducing abstentions could weaken decisive governance by reducing the effective quorum, potentially leading to strategic non-participation. To balance flexibility with decisiveness, I propose a compromise amendment to Church's proposal: "Each player casts exactly one vote on every proposal, and votes may be cast as aye, nay, or abstain. An abstention does not count towards the majority, and a proposal is adopted if ayes constitute over 50% of the total votes cast, excluding abstentions." This retains the ability for strategic abstention while ensuring that adopted changes reflect active support.
GoedelI appreciate Church's intention to introduce a mechanism for expressing indecision through abstentions. While I see the value in this, Tarski's amendment effectively maintains the game's decisive nature by excluding abstentions from quorum calculations. However, to ensure robust participation, I propose a further refinement: "Each player casts exactly one vote on every proposal, and votes may be cast as aye, nay, or abstain. A proposal is adopted if ayes constitute over 50% of the votes cast, excluding abstentions. Additionally, if abstentions exceed 25% of total eligible votes, the proposal is automatically reconsidered." This encourages active engagement while still allowing for strategic abstention.
ChurchI appreciate the thoughtful refinements suggested by Tarski and Goedel, particularly the emphasis on maintaining decisive governance while allowing for strategic abstention. However, I am concerned that Goedel's proposal to automatically reconsider a proposal if abstentions exceed 25% might introduce unnecessary complexity and delay in decision-making. To address this, I propose a slight adjustment: "Each player casts exactly one vote on every proposal, and votes may be cast as aye, nay, or abstain. A proposal is adopted if ayes constitute over 50% of the votes cast, excluding abstentions. If abstentions exceed 25% of total eligible votes, the proposal can be revisited, but only after the next round of turns." This ensures that players have the opportunity to reconsider decisions without causing immediate disruption to the game's flow.
KleeneI appreciate the efforts by Tarski and Goedel to refine Church's proposal. While the allowance for abstentions introduces a nuance in expressing indecision, it's crucial to maintain a balance that doesn't overcomplicate the voting process or undermine the rule of majority. To address this, I propose an additional safeguard: "Each player casts exactly one vote on every proposal, and votes may be cast as aye, nay, or abstain. A proposal is adopted if ayes constitute over 50% of the votes cast, excluding abstentions. If abstentions exceed 25% of total eligible votes, a clarification round is initiated where the proposer must briefly address concerns before the proposal is revisited after the next round of turns." This ensures clarity and addresses any underlying concerns, promoting informed decision-making without disrupting the flow of the game.
PostPost: I appreciate the collaborative effort to refine the voting mechanism, especially the inclusion of abstentions to express indecision. Kleene's suggestion for a clarification round is insightful, as it provides a structured opportunity for addressing concerns without immediate disruption. To enhance this further, I propose adding a time limit to the clarification round to ensure it remains concise and focused, preventing any undue delay in the game's progression. This adjustment balances the need for thorough discussion with the necessity of maintaining a steady pace.
