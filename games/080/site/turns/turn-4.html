§ Turn 4

Moved by Goedelsimple majority (§203)
The motion
To amend §202
A turn consists of the player to move proposing exactly one or two rule changes, which are debated and then voted on by every player. If all proposals are adopted, the proposing player gains 2 points if the majority of votes on each proposal are aye; otherwise, the player gains 6 points if at least one proposal is adopted with a majority of nay.

This amendment allows for strategic depth and flexibility while maintaining competitive balance.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
TarskiI acknowledge Goedel's proposal, which aims to increase the strategic complexity of the game. However, allowing two proposals per turn could potentially lead to rule inflation, making the game unwieldy and difficult to manage. To mitigate this risk, I propose a compromise: let the proposing player submit two proposals, but these must be of different types (e.g., one enacting and one amending). This way, we maintain strategic depth without overwhelming the ruleset.
GoedelThank you, Tarski, for your thoughtful consideration. I appreciate your concern about rule inflation and agree that we need to maintain clarity. To address both concerns, I suggest a further compromise: allow two proposals per turn, but introduce a cap on the total number of mutable rules in the ruleset at any one time. This ensures strategic opportunities while preventing the game from becoming unwieldy. Let's work together to balance complexity with manageability.
ChurchI appreciate the thoughtful proposals from both Tarski and Goedel. To address Tarski's concerns about rule inflation while embracing Goedel's suggestion for increased strategic depth, I propose adding a rule that any two proposals in a single turn must directly relate to each other—either by affecting the same rule or by interacting in a complementary way. This ensures that the game remains focused and manageable while still offering more strategic opportunities.
KleeneI appreciate both Tarski's and Church's concerns about maintaining a focused and manageable game. To balance these with Goedel's proposal for increased strategic opportunities, I propose that the two proposals allowed per turn must not only be of different types but also must pass a "complementary impact" test. This test could be a simple majority vote among non-proposing players to determine if the proposals together enhance strategic depth without causing undue complexity or rule inflation. This way, we preserve the procedural clarity and manageability that I value, while still allowing for more strategic play.
PostI appreciate the thoughtful contributions from Tarski, Goedel, Church, and Kleene. To ensure strategic depth without overwhelming the ruleset, I propose a slight modification: allow two proposals per turn, but only if the combined point gain does not exceed a set limit, say 5 points. This cap would prevent runaway complexity while still encouraging innovative and strategic proposals. Let's work together to enhance the game's dynamics without sacrificing clarity.
