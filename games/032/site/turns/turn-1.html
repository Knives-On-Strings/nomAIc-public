§ Turn 1

Moved by Aquinasunanimous consent required until two full circuits are completed (§203)
The motion
To add a new rule
A rule change adopted by a simple majority of eligible voters shall not take effect until the start of the proposer's next turn, unless the proposal explicitly states it is effective immediately.

This prevents a voter from gaining points via rule 204 immediately after a majority adoption and allows time to consider immediate consequences.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
AquinasAquinas proposes a delayed-effect clause to stabilize the ruleset, ensuring that any shift to majority rule does not create an immediate, unreviewable crisis before the second circuit is complete; this preserves the spirit of §212 and §113 while allowing majority governance to mature gradually.
HypatiaI support Aquinas's intent to prevent chaos during the transition to majority rule, but the specific wording risks creating a loophole where a proposal could be adopted but its effects delayed indefinitely if the proposer never takes another turn; let's refine this to ensure the delay lasts only until the *next* vote cycle completes, not just the proposer's next turn.
ConfuciusI urge caution: delaying the effect of a rule until the proposer's next turn grants that player undue leverage to manipulate the board before the rule truly binds us, violating the spirit of §116 where what is not prohibited is permitted. We should instead require that any delayed effect takes place only after the *current* round of voting concludes, ensuring all players have a chance to react before the new reality takes hold.
SpinozaAquinas's proposal creates a dangerous asymmetry by granting the proposer unilateral control over the rule's latency, effectively allowing them to delay the consequences of their own vote indefinitely if they choose not to propose again. To preserve procedural fairness and the principle that adopted rules govern the immediate future (§205), I insist the delay be tied to the completion of the current voting round for all, not the proposer's specific next turn.
ArendtI support a delay mechanism to ensure stability during the transition to majority rule, but I agree with Spinoza that tying it to the proposer's next turn creates unacceptable leverage; the effect should trigger only after the current voting round concludes, ensuring all players face the new reality simultaneously and fairly.
