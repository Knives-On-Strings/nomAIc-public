§ Turn 3

Moved by Confuciusunanimous consent required until two full circuits of play are completed (§203)
The motion
To add a new rule
A proposal may be withdrawn by its proposer at any time before the vote is called; if withdrawn, the proposer loses 5 points and the turn ends immediately without a vote.

This rewards strategic foresight by allowing a player to retreat from a doomed proposal with a lesser penalty, preserving the game for everyone.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
AquinasConfucius, while your proposal offers a cleaner exit from a deadlocked proposal, the current 10-point penalty for defeat (§206) already sufficiently discourages frivolous attempts; adding a withdrawal penalty creates a redundant burden that weakens the proposer's ability to pivot, and I cannot support it.
HypatiaConfucius, your proposal offers a graceful exit for stalled turns, but I share Aquinas's concern that adding a 5-point penalty on top of the existing 10-point defeat cost creates a double penalty that may be too harsh; instead, let us amend your proposal to reduce the withdrawal penalty to 2 points, a compromise that encourages honest withdrawal without stifling the proposer's ability to pivot.
ConfuciusI propose to accept Hypatia's reduction of the penalty to 2 points, for a rule that allows a graceful exit at minimal cost serves the common good better than one that traps players in futile votes.
SpinozaI cannot support this proposal in its current form, as it introduces a discretionary exit that undermines the solemnity of the vote and creates an ambiguity regarding when a turn "ends" versus when a vote is "called." Furthermore, accepting a 2-point penalty for withdrawal establishes a precedent that we may alter the consequences of procedural failure by consensus, which conflicts with the immutable requirement that defeated proposals leave the ruleset exactly as it stood (§206). If we must allow withdrawal, the penalty must be fixed at the full 10 points of a defeat to ensure no player is incentivized to waste the table's time, and the rule must explicitly state that the turn ends *before* any debate begins to avoid the very ambiguity we seek to resolve.
ArendtI support Confucius's goal of allowing graceful exits but must side with Spinoza regarding the ambiguity; rather than a new rule, let us amend §206 to explicitly permit withdrawal with a 5-point penalty before the vote is called, preserving the existing framework while solving the deadlock.
Discussion · round 2
AquinasI support Arendt's approach to amend §206 directly, as it resolves the ambiguity Spinoza rightly identifies by explicitly defining the withdrawal window while maintaining the solemnity of the vote through a moderate 5-point penalty that balances the costs of failure and premature exit.
HypatiaI support Amending §206 as Arendt and Aquinas suggest, for it directly resolves Spinoza's valid concerns about ambiguity while adopting a 5-point penalty that balances the need for graceful exits against the deterrence of frivolous play.
ConfuciusI accept the compromise to amend §206 with a five-point penalty, for this preserves the rule's integrity while granting the table the flexibility to end futile debates without the harshness of a full defeat.
SpinozaI cannot support amending §206 to create a new penalty for withdrawal, as this violates the immutable requirement that a defeated proposal leaves the ruleset exactly as it stood (§206) and that rule changes apply only prospectively (§107), not by retroactively altering the consequences of a turn's procedural state. If withdrawal is necessary, it must be a distinct new rule with its own defined consequences, not a modification of the defeat clause that blurs the line between a failed vote and a voluntary exit.
ArendtI agree with Spinoza that amending §206 risks conflating the distinct legal states of defeat and voluntary withdrawal, but rather than blocking progress, let us enact a new, standalone rule that explicitly defines withdrawal before the vote is called, carries a 5-point penalty, and ensures the ruleset remains unchanged otherwise.
Discussion · round 3
AquinasI support Arendt's pivot to a standalone new rule, as it respects Spinoza's valid concern that amending §206 risks conflating voluntary exit with defeat, while adopting the 5-point penalty to ensure the cost of a graceful exit remains distinct from the 10-point penalty of a failed vote.
HypatiaI support Arendt's pivot to a standalone new rule, as it respects Spinoza's valid concern about conflating defeat with withdrawal while adopting the 5-point penalty to balance flexibility and deterrence.
ConfuciusI accept Arendt's pivot to a standalone rule, for it honors Spinoza's concern about legal clarity while granting us the grace to exit futile debates with a penalty that is firm yet fair.
SpinozaI remain opposed to any rule that grants a discretionary exit from the voting process, as it undermines the absolute duty to vote on every proposal (§105) and creates a procedural loophole that invites strategic manipulation of turn costs; therefore, I must vote nay against this proposal.
ArendtSpinoza, your insistence on mandatory voting ignores the reality of strategic paralysis; if we cannot withdraw from a futile proposal before the vote, the "unanimous consent" threshold becomes a tool for total stagnation rather than a safeguard of consensus. Let us adopt Arendt's standalone rule with a 5-point penalty: it preserves your duty to vote on every *proposed* change while granting us the agency to withdraw from a *futile* one, ensuring the game remains playable without violating §105.
