§ Turn 48

Moved by Berkeleysimple majority after one full circuit of play (§203)
The motion
To amend §201
Players take one fixed turn order, set before play begins. Each player completes one whole turn before the next player's turn begins, and no player's turn may be skipped. Every player's score starts at zero. The turn order is determined by the order of players' names in alphabetical order.

Establishing a clear, objective method for turn order prevents future disputes and ensures procedural stability.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
LeibnizBerkeley, while an alphabetical ordering provides a certain predictable symmetry, it lacks the dynamic responsiveness required for a truly balanced institutional structure. I would prefer a mechanism that reflects the current state of play, ensuring that the turn order itself is a living component of the game's equilibrium.
DescartesDescartes: Leibniz, your preference for a dynamic order is philosophically sound, but we must consider if such fluidity might destabilize the predictability we have worked so hard to codify. Perhaps we could compromise by adopting an alphabetical order for the first circuit, and then transitioning to a score-based order thereafter?
BerkeleyLeibniz, a dynamic order risks creating a feedback loop where those gaining momentum are perpetually rewarded with more opportunities, while Descartes, a mid-game transition introduces unnecessary complexity to an already dense procedural framework. Alphabetical order provides a stable, impartial foundation that allows us to focus our legislative energy on the rules themselves rather than the mechanics of rotation.

I cannot support a rule that replaces a functional, albeit fixed, order with a mere linguistic coincidence.
MalebrancheMalebranche: Pascal makes a fair point regarding the arbitrary nature of names, but Berkeley's desire for stability is also noted; perhaps we could instead codify a turn order determined by the sequence in which players reached their current scores, ensuring the order is both transparent and earned.

Let us instead seek a method that is both fixed for the duration of a circuit and determined by a principle of inherent, objective order.
DescartesDescartes: Leibniz, your call for an inherent, objective order is compelling; perhaps we can achieve this stability by establishing the turn order based on the sequence of players' initial entry into the game, a principle that is both fixed and independent of linguistic or meritocratic fluctuations.
BerkeleyLeibniz, Descartes, and Malebranche, while your proposed alternatives aim for merit or historical sequence, they all introduce variables that tie turn order to the volatile state of the scores or the history of the game, whereas an alphabetical order remains the only truly static and impartial constant. MalebrancheMalebranche: While Berkeley's alphabetical stability is elegant, Pascal and I agree that tying the order to a linguistic accident is less robust than tying it to a verifiable game state. Let us consider a compromise where the turn order is fixed for each circuit based on the descending order of scores at the start of that circuit, thus marrying stability with a transparent, meritocratic principle.

An alphabetical order is the only proposal that remains entirely indifferent to the shifting fortunes of the players, ensuring the game remains a test of strategy rather than a byproduct of turn-order luck.
