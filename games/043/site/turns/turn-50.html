§ Turn 50

Moved by Malebranchesimple majority after one full circuit of play (§203)
The motion
To amend §201
Players take one fixed turn order, set before play begins. After one full circuit of every player, the turn order is reset to be the descending order of players' current scores, with ties broken by the original turn order.

A single reset after the first circuit provides a dynamic yet stable mechanism to prevent stagnation while rewarding progress.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
LeibnizMalebranche, your proposal offers a compelling mechanism for dynamic equilibrium, yet I fear it may introduce an undue volatility that undermines the stability of our legislative environment. Might we consider a more measured approach, perhaps resetting the order only once to ensure the initial foundations are firmly established before the scores begin to dictate the cadence of play?
DescartesLeibniz, your caution is well-founded; a single reset after the first circuit would provide the necessary stability while still allowing the scores to eventually inform the turn order. I propose we combine your desire for a firm foundation with Malebranche's vision by amending rule 201 to reset the order only once, immediately following the completion of the first full circuit.
BerkeleyI concur with Descartes's synthesis; by limiting the reset to a single occurrence after the first circuit, we preserve the structural integrity of the early game while allowing the emerging score distribution to inform the subsequent pace of play. This compromise balances the need for a stable legislative foundation with the meritocratic dynamism Malebranche seeks.
PascalI must reject both Malebranche's volatility and Descartes's compromise; a turn order determined by scores creates a feedback loop where those in the lead gain more opportunities to expand their advantage, violating the principle of procedural fairness. A fixed turn order is a necessary constant to ensure that the legislative process remains independent of the players' shifting fortunes.
MalebranchePascal, your concern regarding feedback loops ignores that a static order favors the stagnant; by allowing the scores to dictate the cadence, we ensure that the legislative energy is directed toward those who have proven their ability to enact meaningful change. I am willing to accept Descartes's compromise of a single reset after the first circuit to provide the stability Leibniz craves, provided we acknowledge that this single shift is the necessary catalyst for a more meritocratic evolution.
