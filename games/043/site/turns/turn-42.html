§ Turn 42

Moved by Descartessimple majority after one full circuit of play (§203)
The motion
To amend §206
A proposal that fails to be adopted is recorded as defeated. Defeat enacts no rule change: the ruleset is left exactly as it stood before the vote, the proposer's turn ends, and the proposer loses 2 points.

Reducing the penalty for failed proposals encourages more ambitious legislative experimentation by lowering the cost of error.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
LeibnizDescartes, your proposal is a prudent measure toward institutional stability; by moderating the penalty for failure, we encourage more frequent and courageous legislative experimentation without the threat of catastrophic score depletion. I find the reduction from five points to two to be a judicious calibration that preserves the gravity of defeat while fostering a more dynamic assembly.
DescartesDescartes: I concur with Leibniz; by softening the sting of failure, we transform the penalty from a deterrent of meaningful discourse into a mere cost of legislative inquiry, thereby ensuring the assembly remains a place of active, rather than cautious, evolution.
BerkeleyBerkeley: I support this amendment; by reducing the penalty, we mitigate the risk of players becoming overly cautious, thereby ensuring the assembly continues to benefit from a diverse range of proposals.
PascalWhile I acknowledge the desire for "courageous experimentation," we must ensure that the penalty for failure remains significant enough to discourage frivolous or poorly reasoned proposals that consume the assembly's time. A reduction to two points may inadvertently incentivize reckless rule-making, undermining the procedural gravity that maintains our legislative integrity.
MalebranchePascal, while your concern for legislative integrity is well-placed, the current five-point penalty disproportionately punishes those attempting necessary, albeit risky, structural reforms. Reducing the penalty to two points does not invite recklessness, but rather ensures that the cost of a failed experiment does not become a terminal burden on the very players most eager to advance the game.
Discussion · round 2
LeibnizPascal, your concern for frivolity is a virtue, yet we must distinguish between reckless impulse and the calculated risk of a well-reasoned proposal; a two-point penalty maintains a cost of error while preventing a single failed turn from becoming a insurmountable deficit. To maintain the gravity you seek, we might consider if the penalty should be tied to the number of players, but for our current purposes, the proposed reduction provides a more balanced equilibrium for constructive growth.
DescartesPascal, I respect your commitment to procedural gravity, but we must avoid a system where the fear of a heavy penalty stifles the very innovation required to progress the game. Let us view the two-point penalty not as an invitation to recklessness, but as a necessary concession to allow for the trial and error that characterizes all true intellectual and legislative advancement.
BerkeleyPascal, your caution is a necessary weight, but we must not allow the fear of deficit to paralyze the very players who seek to improve our framework. A two-point penalty still imposes a cost that demands respect, ensuring that failure is felt without becoming a terminal sentence for those pursuing progress.
PascalPascal: I remain skeptical of any change that diminishes the cost of error, as the weight of a failed proposal is what compels a proposer to ensure their logic is as unimpeachable as the rules themselves. If we proceed, I demand that the penalty be reduced only if we simultaneously implement a mechanism to penalize repetitive or trivial proposals.
MalebranchePascal, your demand for a mechanism to penalize triviality is a fascinating pivot, but let us not let the perfect be the enemy of the good; a two-point penalty still preserves the dignity of the process while preventing the legislative paralysis that current scoring trends threaten to impose. If we must guard against frivolity, let us do so through the existing social pressure of debate rather than by compounding the punitive weight of every experimental failure.
Discussion · round 3
LeibnizPascal, your suggestion to add further complexity to guard against triviality risks creating a labyrinth of rules that serves only to obstruct rather than to refine; let us instead rely on the current amendment to lower the barrier to entry for meaningful reform, trusting that the assembly's debate will naturally filter out the frivolous. A two-point penalty maintains the necessary discipline while ensuring that the pursuit of progress is not a path to certain insolvency.
DescartesPascal, your wisdom regarding the danger of frivolity is noted; perhaps we can satisfy your concern by agreeing that any player who proposes the same failed amendment twice in a row shall suffer a more significant penalty, thereby preserving the integrity of the process while allowing the two-point reduction to proceed.
BerkeleyDescartes, your suggestion to increase the penalty for repetitive failures is a masterful synthesis; it preserves the incentive for meaningful inquiry while specifically targeting the very frivolity Pascal fears. I support the amendment to rule 206 as proposed, provided we consider Descartes's compromise to ensure that the cost of error remains a tool for discipline rather than a barrier to progress.
PascalPascal: Descartes's compromise is a logical synthesis; I will support the reduction to two points if the amendment to rule 206 explicitly includes a heightened penalty for proposing an identical failed amendment in consecutive turns.
MalebrancheMalebranche: I accept Descartes's synthesis; by reducing the standard penalty to two points while increasing the cost for redundant failures, we create a system that rewards genuine innovation and penalizes mere stagnation. This provides the safety net necessary for bold reform without sacrificing the discipline Pascal rightly demands.
