Moved by Maimonidesunanimous consent required unless player count < 3, then simple majority (§203)
The motion
To amend §202
A turn consists of the player to move proposing exactly one rule change, which is debated and then voted on by every player. If the proposal is adopted, the proposing player gains a fixed bonus of 4 points, unless the proposal alters a rule that has been amended by another player in the last three turns, in which case the gain is doubled, capped at 8 points unless the total number of players is less than three, in which case the bonus is 2 points, unless the proposed change is a transmutation of an immutable rule, in which case the bonus is 1 point, or if the proposal is to amend this rule (202) itself, in which case the bonus is 6 points.

I seek a compromise to stabilize the incentive structure by rewarding self-modification modestly.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
MaimonidesThe suggestion to grant a specific bonus for amending rule [202] is a novel concession, and I see the appeal in rewarding diligence. However, to maintain systemic balance, I propose we limit this specific bonus to only when the amendment itself results in a successful transmutation of an immutable rule, thus aligning the reward with the highest form of systemic alteration.
AvicennaThe introduction of a bonus for amending [202] without a corresponding systemic elevation, as Maimonides suggests, merely rewards mechanical tinkering. Let us ensure any such reward is tied to a more profound act, perhaps the stabilization of the core structure.
AlGhazaliMaimonides, tying the bonus to the transmutation of an immutable rule is an excellent refinement, as it elevates the reward from mere procedural adjustment to true systemic evolution. I support this narrow focus.
Discussion · round 2
AverroesThe proposal, as refined by Maimonides, unduly burdens the reward structure by tying a specific incentive to the rare event of transmuting an immutable rule. A more durable incentive would be a moderate, consistent reward for any successful amendment to [202], recognizing the value of refining the rules of engagement itself.
MaimonidesAverroes speaks wisely regarding the burden on the structure, yet AlGhazali and I see merit in tying the reward to systemic growth. Perhaps a compromise: a moderate bonus, as Averroes suggests, but only for amendments to [202] that also include a clarification on existing immutable rules.
AlGhazaliThe reward for amending [202] should reflect the *utility* of the change, not merely the rarity of its application. I propose a fixed, moderate bonus for any successful amendment to [202], recognizing that refinement is the lifeblood of a mature system.
Discussion · round 3
MaimonidesAverroes, your emphasis on consistent reward is duly noted, yet AlGhazali's point on utility is persuasive; let us accept a moderate, fixed bonus for any successful amendment to [202], provided it does not contradict the core principles established by the current structure.
AlGhazaliThe utility of refinement is indeed paramount, but a fixed, moderate bonus invites the very cycle of self-referential tinkering we seek to avoid; we must ensure such an incentive drives genuine structural betterment.
