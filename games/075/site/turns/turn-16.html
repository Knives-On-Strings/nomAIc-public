Moved by Laozisimple majority of those voting (§203)
The motion
To add a new rule
A player who votes aye on a proposal that is adopted may, immediately after the vote, declare that they are transferring all points they would otherwise gain from that proposal’s adoption to any other player of their choice. If they do, the points are transferred instead of being added to their own score. A player may make such a transfer at most once per full circuit of turn order.

This allows voluntary generosity, reinforcing collaborative spirit while respecting the per-circuit cap to prevent excessive point consolidation.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
LaoziI appreciate the spirit of generosity, but this proposal risks creating coercive side-payments that circumvent rule 210’s ban on outside negotiation; a per-circuit cap is insufficient to prevent quid-pro-quo deals that distort debate.
ZhuXiI understand Laozi’s concern, but this transfer is public and limited to once per circuit, which aligns with the collaborative spirit of rule 302 and the already-adopted rule 315. I could support it if we require the transfer declaration to be recorded in the official game log before the next proposal is voted on, ensuring transparency.
WangYangmingI share Laozi’s concern about coercion, but with a public declaration logged before the next vote and the once‑per‑circuit limit, transparency can mitigate that risk. If the wording is amended to require immediate logging, I could support it as a natural extension of the generosity rule 315.
DongZhongshuI oppose any transfer mechanism that could be used as payment for votes, even with transparency. Rule 210 prohibits outside negotiation, but in‑game side‑deals still corrupt debate. I will vote nay.
ShaoYongI agree with Laozi and DongZhongshu — even with logging, this creates a direct vote-for-points market, which corrupts debate. Rule 315 already allows generosity; we don’t need a tool for quid-pro-quo.
Discussion · round 2

DongZhongshuI maintain my opposition: even with post-adoption logging, the mere existence of a transfer mechanism creates a currency for implicit vote-buying, corrupting debate. Rule 315 already covers voluntary generosity; we need not add a rule inviting strategic side-payments.

That creates a shadow market, undermining rule 210’s intent. Rule 315 already covers generosity; we don’t need a parallel channel ripe for abuse. I urge you to reconsider.
