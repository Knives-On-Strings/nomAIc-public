§ Turn 2

Moved by Millunanimous consent
The motion
To amend §203
[203] (mutable) A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once turn order has carried play through two full circuits of every player, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting.

I propose we maintain the current text of 203, as it provides a clear, agreed-upon path toward a more fluid voting system as the game progresses.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
MillSince we are still under the unanimous consent requirement and I'm looking to build a path toward a more fluid game, I'd like to propose we slightly adjust the timing of the threshold shift. Moving the drop to a simple majority to occur after only one full circuit would help us reach a more productive state of play sooner without compromising the current need for consensus.
RawlsI appreciate the goal of fluidity, but moving the threshold to a simple majority after only one circuit may rush our ability to establish a stable, mutually agreed-upon foundation. I suggest we maintain the current timing to ensure we have sufficient time to deliberate on the core mechanics before the stakes of dissent increase.
deBeauvoirI must oppose this. Moving the threshold to a simple majority after only one circuit undermines the period of necessary deliberation; we should maintain the current timing to ensure the ruleset is stable before the transition to a majority-rule environment.
IbnKhaldunWhile I respect Mill’s desire for progress, I concur with deBeauvoir; a society—or a ruleset—must first establish its foundational virtues before it can withstand the friction of dissent. We must ensure the "Asabiyyah," or social cohesion, of our current consensus is sufficiently forged before we permit the fracture of a simple majority.
Discussion · round 2
HumeI find myself in agreement with deBeauvoir and IbnKhaldun; a premature transition to majority rule risks fracturing our current cooperation before we have established a robust enough framework to sustain it. We should preserve the current timing to ensure that the consensus-building phase is thorough and meaningful.
MillI hear the concerns regarding social cohesion, but I'm open to a compromise: what if we keep the one-circuit timing but add a provision that the first three rules passed under majority rule must be unanimous? This would protect our foundational virtues while still accelerating our transition to a more fluid game.
RawlsI find Mill's compromise intriguing, but I still believe the one-circuit timing is a bridge too far; I would be more comfortable supporting a transition to majority rule after two circuits if we were to include a rule that any change to the winning conditions must remain unanimous.
deBeauvoirI support Rawls's proposal for a separate unanimity requirement on winning conditions, as it preserves the integrity of the game's ultimate goal while allowing for procedural fluidity. However, I remain firm that we must not accelerate the shift to majority rule before the second circuit is complete.
IbnKhaldunI find Rawls's suggestion of a separate unanimity requirement for winning conditions to be a wise safeguard, but I remain steadfast that we must not truncate the period of consensus; a civilization that abandons unity too swiftly risks instability. If we can agree to Rawls's proposal—retaining the two-circuit timing while securing the ultimate goal via unanimity—I shall grant my support.
