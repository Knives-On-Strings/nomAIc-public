The motion
To amend §212
Disputes over whether a rule applies as written, or whether a move was legal, are resolved through the table's appeal procedure: any player may dispute a decisive ruling, and a vote of all seated players decides whether that ruling stands or is overturned. If a dispute arises, the proposing player can seek a consensus-based resolution from teammates, allowing for a collaborative approach to conflict resolution.

Facilitates smoother gameplay by encouraging teamwork and collaboration in resolving disputes.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
TarskiWhile I appreciate the intent to foster teamwork through a consensus-based resolution, I must express concern that this amendment might undermine the integrity of rule adjudication by introducing an element of negotiation that could skew outcomes. The appeal procedure should remain impartial and ensure decisions are based solely on the merits of the dispute, rather than the persuasive power of a proposing player's teammates. To maintain fairness, I propose retaining the current impartial vote-based resolution mechanism.
GoedelThank you, Tarski, for your thoughtful concerns. I understand the importance of maintaining fairness and impartiality in the appeal process. Perhaps we can find a middle ground by allowing the consensus-based resolution as a secondary option only if the initial vote does not reach a majority decision. This way, we preserve impartiality while still enabling collaboration when absolutely necessary. Could we agree to this compromise?
ChurchI appreciate Goedel's willingness to find a middle ground. However, I am concerned that even a secondary consensus-based resolution could introduce bias. To safeguard fairness, I suggest an alternative: if the initial vote does not reach a majority, the dispute should be escalated to an impartial mediator chosen randomly from the players. This way, we ensure an unbiased resolution while still addressing the need for a decision when a majority is not achieved.
KleeneKleene: I see merit in Church's suggestion for an impartial mediator, yet I'm concerned about the randomness potentially leading to disputes over mediator choice. To maintain procedural fairness and clarity, how about we stipulate that if the initial vote fails to reach a majority, the next two seated players take turns proposing an impartial mediator from among themselves, with a simple majority deciding the choice? This ensures a transparent and participatory selection process.
PostPost: I appreciate the contributions from everyone. Kleene's proposal for mediator selection offers transparency, but let's ensure fairness by allowing any player, not just the next two seated, to propose a mediator, with a simple majority deciding. This way, we avoid potential biases while ensuring every player has a say in the selection process, maintaining the integrity of the appeal procedure.

KleeneKleene: While I appreciate the intention behind Goedel's proposal for a time-bound mediator decision, I worry that automatically resolving in favor of the status quo may inadvertently disadvantage a party with a legitimate dispute. This keeps the process transparent and fair while ensuring the game progresses smoothly.
