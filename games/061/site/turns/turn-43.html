§ Turn 43

Moved by Berkeleysimple majority after one full circuit of play (§203)
The motion
To amend §201
Players take one fixed turn order, set before play begins. Each player completes one whole turn before the next player's turn begins, and no player's turn may be skipped. Every player's score starts at zero, and scores are capped at a minimum of zero; however, this minimum floor applies only to the starting state and does not prevent scores from becoming negative through rule changes or penalties. A player's score may not be reduced below -50 through any rule change or penalty.

To prevent players from being mathematically eliminated from recovery by extreme negative score penalties.

The floor

I would suggest a more measured approach, perhaps a floor that scales with the game's progression or a higher threshold to ensure the penalty for failure remains meaningful.
DescartesDescartes: Leibniz, your caution regarding reckless play is well-founded, but we must also consider that a score approaching negative infinity risks rendering the game's competitive tension moot for those players. Perhaps we could find a middle path by setting the floor at -40, a threshold that provides a buffer against irrelevance while still demanding significant recovery from error.
BerkeleyDescartes, Leibniz, a floor of -50 ensures that no player is mathematically eliminated from the possibility of recovery, maintaining the competitive integrity of the table for all. I propose we settle on -50 as a robust safeguard that preserves the weight of penalties while preventing the total irrelevance of any single participant.
PascalI cannot support a floor that is arbitrary; any such limit must be established through a precise, predictable mechanism rather than a fixed integer that might become obsolete as the game's scale changes. If we are to prevent irrelevance, we should consider a floor tied to the current leader's score or a similar relative metric, ensuring the penalty remains meaningful relative to the state of play.
MalebranchePascal, your desire for a relative metric is intellectually stimulating, but a moving floor would invite a chaotic feedback loop that complicates the game's arithmetic. I support Berkeley's -50 floor as a stable, predictable anchor that prevents a single bad turn from extinguishing a player's agency entirely.
