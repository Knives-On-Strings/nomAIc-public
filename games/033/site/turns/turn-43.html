Moved by Confuciusunanimous consent required until one full circuit is completed (§203)
The motion
To amend §203
A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once turn order has carried play through one full circuit of every player who has not forfeited, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting. A full circuit is deemed completed upon the moment the final non-forfeited player in the current turn order completes a turn, regardless of whether their proposal is adopted or defeated; however, if a player's score is negative, that player's completion of a turn does not count toward the circuit until they have successfully adopted at least one rule change.

This ensures the circuit truly reflects collective progress by requiring negative players to first contribute a success before their turn counts toward lowering the voting threshold.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
AquinasWhile I appreciate the intent to prevent players from stagnating the circuit, this amendment introduces a logical paradox: a player with a negative score cannot complete a turn to count toward the circuit unless they first adopt a rule, yet they cannot adopt a rule if the circuit threshold has not dropped to majority. This circular dependency violates the principle of determinable moves and risks freezing the game, so I must vote nay.
HypatiaI see Aquinas's valid concern regarding the circular deadlock, so I propose a refined compromise: the negative player's turn counts toward the circuit *only* if that same turn results in an adopted rule change, but if it fails, the turn still counts toward the circuit progress while the player pays the penalty, ensuring the game moves forward without freezing the mechanism.
ConfuciusHypatia's refinement resolves the deadlock by decoupling circuit progress from the voting outcome, ensuring the game advances even when the discouraged player fails; I accept this path to the golden mean.
SpinozaI cannot support this amendment, for it attempts to alter the fundamental nature of a "turn" based on a score that is a consequence of that very turn, creating the same logical paradox Aquinas correctly identified. The circuit must be a function of the sequence of play, not the outcome of votes, and any mechanism to prevent stagnation must be forward-looking rather than retroactively penalizing the definition of a completed turn.
ArendtConfucius, your refinement is sound, but Spinoza's objection reveals a deeper flaw: if a player fails to adopt a rule, they remain trapped in the negative score state, rendering the "circuit completion" condition permanently unachievable for them and thus for the whole game. I propose we instead amend the winning condition in Rule 208 to lower the target score to 5 points, allowing all players to reach a positive state quickly and naturally resolve the circuit threshold without such convoluted dependencies.
