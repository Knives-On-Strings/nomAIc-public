Moved by Confuciusunanimous consent required until one full circuit is completed (§203)
The motion
To amend §203
A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once turn order has carried play through one full circuit of every player who has not forfeited, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting. A full circuit is deemed completed upon the moment the final non-forfeited player in the current turn order completes a turn, regardless of whether their proposal is adopted or defeated, provided at least one proposal was adopted during that circuit.

We must ensure that a circuit of silence does not trigger the majority threshold, for a legislature that produces no laws is no legislature at all.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
AquinasI shall oppose Confucius's amendment, for attaching the threshold drop to the adoption of a proposal invites paralysis: if a single player consistently blocks every measure, the game may stall indefinitely without ever reaching the simple majority. We require the passage of time to break gridlock, not the caprice of unanimous adoption.
HypatiaI oppose this amendment as it reintroduces the paralysis Aquinas rightly fears, allowing a single holdout to freeze the game indefinitely; instead, let us propose a compromise that drops the threshold after a fixed number of *completed turns* regardless of adoption count, ensuring progress without sacrificing the spirit of consensus.
ConfuciusI hear your concern, but the current rule allows gridlock even when every player has spoken, whereas my amendment ensures the threshold drops only when the table has actually achieved consensus on at least one matter, proving the circuit is functional rather than merely passing time. If we fear a single holdout, let us trust that a true majority will eventually form once the circuit proves its worth, rather than resetting the rules based on mere attendance.
SpinozaI oppose this amendment because it permits a single player to indefinitely freeze the legislative mechanism, violating the principle that the rules must remain operable for all; the current text correctly ensures progress through time itself rather than hostage to a consensus that may never form.
