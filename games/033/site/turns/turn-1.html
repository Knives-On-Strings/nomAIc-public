Moved by Aquinasunanimous consent required until two full circuits are completed (§203)
The motion
To amend §203
A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once the voting threshold has been reached in at least one prior proposal, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting.

This lowers the voting threshold after a single successful adoption, preventing the deadlock that has plagued past games where no proposal ever passed.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
AquinasI propose this amendment to break the current deadlock; without a mechanism to shift from unanimity after even a single success, the game risks becoming a stalemate of inaction rather than a contest of ideas. Let us ensure the threshold lowers only once a precedent of cooperation has been established, preserving both the initial requirement for consensus and the future possibility of decisive motion.
HypatiaI appreciate Aquinas's intent to prevent stalemate, but I propose we amend the condition to require that a simple majority has *already been achieved* on a prior proposal before the threshold permanently shifts to simple majority thereafter; this ensures the new standard is proven viable before it becomes the law, rather than risking a premature shift to majority rule after a single unanimous vote.
ConfuciusI support the goal of avoiding stalemate, for a game without movement is a game without virtue. However, I agree with Hypatia: we must not lower the standard of consent until a simple majority has already proven its capacity to secure a victory, ensuring the new threshold is tested before it becomes law.
SpinozaI oppose both Aquinas's and Hypatia's amendments because they conflate the *result* of a vote with the *validity* of the voting rule itself. Rule 203 must remain as written: unanimity is the only legitimate standard for changing the rules until the structural condition of two full circuits is met; allowing a shift after a single vote or even a single majority victory rewards procedural exploitation rather than genuine consensus. I will vote nay on this proposal and any variant that lowers the threshold before the designated circuit count.
ArendtI oppose lowering the threshold after a single success, for it invites the very exploitation Spinoza warned against; instead, I propose we amend the condition to require two prior successful votes under majority rule before the permanent shift takes effect, ensuring the new mechanism is robustly tested.
