§ Turn 8

Moved by Confuciusunanimous consent required until one full circuit of players has voted (§203)
The motion
To amend §203
A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once turn order has carried play through one full circuit of every player, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting. However, if any player's score is negative, that player may propose an amendment to lower the voting threshold to a simple majority immediately, which takes effect upon adoption.

This allows players with negative scores to break the deadlock of unanimous consent, restoring fairness and the game's progress for the common good.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
AquinasI oppose Confucius's proposal as a breach of institutional stability; allowing a negative score to unilaterally collapse unanimous consent invites chaos and punishes the very players most in need of the game's order. Let us first endure the initial circuit to test if cooperation can sustain the threshold naturally before introducing such a destabilizing shortcut.
HypatiaWhile Aquinas rightly warns against chaos, Confucius's proposal offers a vital safety valve for players trapped by early bad luck, preventing the game from stalling before the natural circuit ends; I support this amendment as a compromise that preserves the unanimous threshold for cooperative play while allowing negative scorers to propose a shift to majority rule, provided they build consensus for it.
ConfuciusHypatia is correct: the unanimous threshold is a noble ideal, but without a safety valve, a single early misfortune can strangle the game for everyone, violating the common good. I propose we accept this amendment not to break unity, but to ensure that the circuit completes; if a player is negative, they must still win over the table to change the rules, preserving the spirit of consent while removing the paralysis of despair.
SpinozaI oppose this amendment; while the fear of paralysis is real, allowing negative scores to unilaterally trigger a shift to majority rule rewards misfortune and creates a procedural loophole where a single player's despair overrides the collective's right to the full circuit of deliberation. We must uphold the integrity of the unanimous threshold for the first round, as rule 116 permits any player to propose alternative solutions later without compromising the current order.
ArendtConfucius, your safety valve is sound, but Aquinas and Spinoza rightly fear that letting negative scorers trigger the shift immediately rewards bad luck and bypasses the necessary circuit of trust; instead, I propose we amend the rule to allow negative scorers to *propose* the change at any time, but require the vote to actually pass through the standard unanimous threshold before it takes effect, preserving their agency without breaking the first circuit's integrity.
