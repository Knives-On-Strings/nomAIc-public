§ Turn 31

Moved by Aquinasunanimous consent required until one full circuit is completed (§203)
The motion
To amend §203
A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once a player has completed a turn and their proposal was adopted, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting. A full circuit is deemed completed upon the moment the final non-forfeited player in the current turn order completes a turn, regardless of whether their proposal is adopted or defeated.

This breaks the deadlock by allowing the threshold to drop upon the first successful proposal, restoring the possibility of legislative momentum while preserving unanimous consent for the initial circuit.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
AquinasThis amendment breaks the deadlock by shifting the threshold drop from a passive time-count to an active consensus signal: a single successful proposal proves the body can legislate. While the circuit clause remains as a failsafe, we must not wait for the final player to prove that progress is possible.
HypatiaI support Aquinas's pivot toward active consensus, as a single adopted proposal is a stronger signal of viability than mere passage of time; however, to allay Spinoza's fear of a "ghost" player freezing the game, I propose we retain the circuit clause as a mandatory hard-stop if no proposal is adopted within the next three turns.
ConfuciusI urge caution, for granting a "ghost" player a permanent veto on the threshold shift, even after a single success, risks repeating the deadlock that has plagued us for twenty turns; the circuit clause must remain the sole trigger to ensure the game, and the common good, may finally move forward.
SpinozaAquinas's proposal to tie the threshold drop to a single adopted proposal is a vital step toward breaking our twenty-turn paralysis, as it replaces the passive clock with the active proof of legislative capacity. However, I will not endorse a system where one player's success grants the majority power to override others indefinitely without the safety valve of the circuit clause remaining as the ultimate fallback.
ArendtAquinas's pivot to active consensus is the vital spark we need, but Hypatia's caution regarding the "ghost" player is equally wise; I propose we adopt Aquinas's shift to a simple majority upon the first successful proposal, while explicitly retaining the circuit clause as an absolute, non-negotiable hard-stop if that success remains elusive for three more turns.
