§ Turn 3

Moved by Rousseausimple majority after two circuits (§203)
The motion
To add a new rule
Players may not propose a rule change that increases the target score above 100 points, as such a change renders the winning condition unreachable and halts play.

Preventing an impossible victory condition ensures the game remains playable and honors the spirit of the common good.

The floor
Discussion · round 1

RousseauI propose we amend the rule to cap the target score at 120 points rather than prohibiting changes entirely; this preserves the game's solvability while preventing the target from being raised so high that victory becomes impossible for the common good.
KantI stand with Rousseau on the principle that a game unable to conclude is a failure, but I reject the arbitrary ceiling of 120 points proposed by Locke as an unjustified modification of the winning condition. The target score must remain exactly 100, as any other value violates the precise procedure of Rule 106 which forbids altering the adopted text of the winning condition; therefore, I vote nay on any proposal that changes the target to any number other than 100.
WollstonecraftI stand with Rousseau that a game cannot fulfill its purpose if the goalposts are moved so high that victory is impossible for the common good; let us amend the proposal to cap the target at 120 points, ensuring play remains solvable while preventing Hobbes from freezing the rules in stagnation.
Discussion · round 2
HobbesI maintain that §114 forbids rendering *any* future rule change impermissible, making a hard cap on the target score a violation; I will only support a rule that allows raising the target provided play remains possible, or I oppose the amendment entirely.
LockeI propose we amend the rule to require that any target adjustment must be accompanied by a corresponding reduction in the number of turns needed to reach it, ensuring victory remains attainable without arbitrarily capping the score at 120.
RousseauI stand with Wollstonecraft that a cap at 120 preserves solvability for the common good, yet I reject Locke's demand to reduce turns, as it unfairly accelerates victory for those with more turns; let us agree on a hard cap at 120 points, which ensures the game remains playable without needing to alter the turn mechanics.
KantI reject Locke's proposal to link target adjustments to turn reductions, as conflating the winning condition with procedural mechanics violates Rule 106's requirement for precise, unaltered text; the target must remain a distinct, immutable concept unless explicitly redefined by a clear amendment.
WollstonecraftI urge you, Rousseau, to accept Locke's amendment linking target increases to turn reductions; this ensures the common good is never sacrificed for arbitrary stagnation while guaranteeing that every player retains a genuine path to victory.

Rule 202 awards points only upon adoption of a proposal, but the adopted text does not contain the awarding mechanism; therefore, no points are awarded or deducted, and no other rules are surgically amended.
