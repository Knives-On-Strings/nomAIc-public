§ Turn 2

Moved by Lockesimple majority after two circuits (§203)
The motion
To amend §203
A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once turn order has carried play through two full circuits of every player, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting, provided at least two players voted aye in the preceding circuit.

This amendment prevents a single saboteur from blocking progress in future circuits by requiring a minimum show of support before the majority threshold applies.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
HobbesThe proposed amendment to Rule 203 is a prudent safeguard against the paralysis of unanimity without sacrificing the stability of early turns. By requiring a consensus baseline before efficiency kicks in, we ensure that no single faction can hijack the game before a stable order is established, aligning perfectly with my commitment to durable institutions. I will support this compromise to prevent the chaos of premature majoritarianism.
LockeI credit Hobbes for recognizing the danger of immediate majoritarianism; I will support this amendment as long as we agree to credit any player who voted against the change in the second circuit with 5 bonus points, ensuring our compromise rewards restraint.
RousseauLocke, your offer to reward restraint with points is a charming incentive, yet it fails to address the core danger: if a simple majority is required after only two circuits, a fleeting coalition can permanently bind the game before the full diversity of our views has been tested. I propose we wait until three full circuits have passed before any majority threshold takes effect, ensuring that the common good is not sacrificed to a transient alliance, and I will support this amendment only if we adopt that stricter timing.
KantI must press for the precision of procedure that defines our liberty; the current text of Rule 203 is already clear, but Locke's amendment introduces a dangerous ambiguity regarding what counts as a "preceding circuit" when votes are split. Unless we can guarantee that the phrase "at least two players voted aye" is a sufficient condition to trigger the majority threshold without creating loopholes for manipulation, I cannot accept this compromise, as I will not support rules I cannot apply consistently against myself.
WollstonecraftThis compromise introduces a dangerous loophole: if the coalition fractures in the second circuit, the rule remains frozen in unanimity, rendering our early turns a theater of futility rather than a path to reform. We must either lower the threshold to two circuits with no precondition, or wait three circuits; a conditional threshold is a trick to stall the very evolution this game demands.
