# The Statute

_As at turn 50._

## Entrenched

**§101.** Every player must, at all times, abide by the ruleset currently in force — including every change adopted since play began.
**§102.** Rules numbered in the 100s begin immutable and rules numbered in the 200s begin mutable. Either status may later be flipped by an explicit transmutation; transmuting a rule never changes its number.
**§103.** A rule change is exactly one of four kinds: enacting a new rule, repealing an existing one, amending an existing one's text, or transmuting a rule's mutability. No other kind of rule change exists.
**§104.** Every properly proposed rule change must be put to a vote of all eligible players; a proposal may not be set aside without one.
**§105.** Every player is an eligible voter, and every eligible voter must cast a vote on every proposal that comes to a vote.
**§106.** A proposal must be written down in the exact words that will be voted on. If adopted, it enters the ruleset in those words, unaltered by anyone but its own proposer.
**§107.** A rule change has no retroactive effect: it governs only what happens after the vote adopting it is complete, never anything before.
**§108.** Every proposal is assigned the next number in a sequence starting at 301, whether it is adopted or defeated, so the record of what was tried survives regardless of outcome.
**§109.** Transmuting a rule's mutability requires the unanimous assent of all eligible voters, and must be proposed and voted on explicitly as a transmutation — never as a side effect of some other change.
**§110.** Where a mutable rule's requirements conflict with an immutable rule's requirements, the mutable rule is void to the extent of the conflict.
**§111.** Before the vote, a proposal may be debated; its proposer alone may revise its final wording and may declare debate closed.
**§112.** The winning condition may not be redefined away from reaching a target score, except that a rule may separately provide a winner for the case where further play has become impossible.
**§113.** Any player may forfeit the game at any time. Forfeiting carries no penalty worse than losing outright.
**§114.** At least one mutable rule must remain in the ruleset at all times, and no rule change may render every future rule change impermissible.
**§115.** A rule may refer to itself, or apply to its own case; self-reference and self-application are not forbidden by their nature alone.
**§116.** Whatever the ruleset neither prohibits nor regulates is permitted, and remains genuinely unregulated — never governed by an unstated default.

## Mutable

**§201.** Players take one fixed turn order, set before play begins. Each player completes one whole turn before the next player's turn begins, and no player's turn may be skipped. Every player's score starts at zero.
**§202.** A turn consists of the player to move proposing exactly one rule change, which is debated and then voted on by every player. If the proposal is adopted, the proposing player gains 2 points from the change. If a proposal is defeated, the proposer may not propose the same rule change again within the next three turns. Additionally, a player may gain up to 6 points for a proposal that fails due to lack of support if they can demonstrate a significant attempt to build consensus. Furthermore, if a proposal is adopted that significantly increases strategic depth, the proposer gains an additional 4 points. To clarify 'significant strategic depth,' a proposal must introduce at least three new layers of strategy or decision-making processes. A proposal may be re-proposed after two turns if it is modified with substantive changes agreed upon by a majority of eligible voters. Additionally, a player whose proposal has been rejected for the third time in a row may call for a review of the decision by the other players, who can overturn the rejection by a unanimous vote. To further encourage collaborative engagement, if a player's cumulative score reaches a multiple of 20, they gain the ability to propose an additional rule change in their next turn, subject to the same conditions as a regular proposal. Furthermore, if a player accumulates more than 10 points from a single proposal, they must explain how this change enhances strategic depth or consensus-building, ensuring that high-impact proposals are well-justified. Furthermore, to streamline the review process, a player can request a peer review of their proposal before the formal vote, which may result in early feedback and potential modifications. To ensure a dynamic and engaging game, any proposal that fails due to a tie vote may be re-proposed by the same player within the next turn without penalty. Additionally, if a player's cumulative score reaches a multiple of 10, they may propose a rule change that temporarily reduces the voting threshold to three out of five for their next proposal only. Additionally, a player who proposes a rule change that significantly enhances strategic depth or consensus-building and is adopted, gains 3 additional points.
**§203.** A rule change is adopted only when a simple majority of eligible voters, defined as three out of five, vote aye, with an additional condition that any rule affecting the voting threshold itself requires a simple majority of four out of five.
**§204.** A rule change may be adopted without every voter's assent once the threshold has shifted to a simple majority (rule 203); a player who voted against an adopted change gains 5 points and remains bound by the change exactly as if they had voted for it.
**§205.** An adopted rule change takes effect immediately after the vote, and the proposer may gain points from the change in their next turn. A proposal that fails to be adopted results in the proposer losing 2 points.
**§206.** An adopted rule change takes effect immediately after the vote, but the proposer may gain points from the change in their next turn, and may not propose the same rule change again within the next three turns.
**§207.** Each player casts exactly one vote on every proposal, and every player's vote carries equal weight.
**§208.** The first player to reach a target score of 91 points wins the game outright.
**§209.** The ruleset may never hold more than 23 mutable rules at one time.
**§210.** Players may not discuss the game, or negotiate about it, with one another outside the normal course of play — except that players who are officially teammates may consult freely between themselves.
**§211.** Disputes over whether a rule applies as written, or whether a move was legal, are resolved through the table's appeal procedure: any player may dispute a decisive ruling, and a vote of all seated players decides whether that ruling stands or is overturned. This rule takes precedence over every other rule that determines a winner, except rule 112.
**§212.** Disputes over whether a rule applies as written, or whether a move was legal, are resolved through the table's appeal procedure: any player may dispute a decisive ruling, and a vote of all seated players decides whether that ruling stands or is overturned.
**§213.** If the ruleset is changed so that further play is impossible, or if a move's legality cannot be determined with finality, or if a move appears equally legal and illegal by the best reasoning available and not overruled, then the first player unable to complete a turn wins the game outright. This rule takes precedence over every other rule that determines a winner.
