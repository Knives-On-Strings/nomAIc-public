§ Turn 4

Moved by Goedelsimple majority (§203)
The motion
To amend §208
The first player to reach a target score of 120 points wins the game outright.

Extending the target score provides more strategic depth and opportunities for all players to influence the outcome.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
TarskiI appreciate the proposal by Goedel to adjust the winning condition to a target score of 120 points. While I recognize the strategic shift this might introduce, I must express concern that this amendment could unduly extend the game's duration, possibly leading to diminishing returns in player engagement and strategic depth. To address these concerns while still opening the door for strategic evolution, I propose a middle ground: let's amend [208] to read, "The first player to reach a target score of 110 points wins the game outright." This compromise retains the spirit of extending the play but keeps the game within a more manageable and strategic timeframe. Additionally, to balance this change and ensure it does not overly favor any single player or strategy disproportionately, I suggest we consider revisiting rule [204] in parallel discussions to adjust the point differential gained or lost through voting outcomes. This could help maintain competitive balance and encourage a more dynamic strategy landscape throughout the extended play period. I look forward to the table's thoughts on these suggestions and remain open to further negotiation to reach a consensus that benefits all players strategically.
GoedelI appreciate Tarski's willingness to find a compromise with the target score adjustment to 110 points. However, I would like to propose a slightly different approach to maintain strategic complexity while addressing the concerns about game length. Let's amend [208] to state: "The first player to reach a target score of 115 points wins the game outright." This offers a middle ground that extends the game beyond the original target, allowing for more strategic depth and opportunities for rule changes, but is not as prolonged as the 120-point suggestion. To further ensure fair play and engagement, I propose we add a new rule: "If a player reaches a score of 100 points, they gain the ability to propose two rule changes in their next turn, rather than one." This incentivizes reaching the initial target score while also providing an interesting strategic twist for those who reach it first. I am open to further discussions and adjustments to these proposals to ensure they align with the table's strategic interests and maintain a competitive and engaging game environment. Goedel, looking forward to your thoughts and any further negotiations to refine these proposals for mutual benefit.
ChurchI appreciate the thoughtful compromise proposed by Tarski and Goedel regarding the target score adjustment to 115 points. This strikes a balance between extending the game for strategic exploration and maintaining a reasonable timeframe. Furthermore, Goedel's suggestion to allow a player who reaches 100 points to propose two rule changes in their next turn adds an intriguing layer of strategy. It rewards progress towards the initial target while encouraging dynamic rule proposals, which could lead to more engaging gameplay. To ensure these changes do not disproportionately benefit any single player, I propose we also amend rule [204] to adjust the point differential for voting outcomes. Specifically, let's change the penalty for voting against an adopted change from 10 points to 5 points. This adjustment maintains competitive balance by reducing the impact of a single vote while still rewarding consensus. I am open to further discussions and adjustments to refine these proposals for the benefit of all players at the table. Let's work together to ensure these changes enhance the strategic depth and fairness of the game.
KleeneI appreciate the efforts by Tarski and Goedel to adjust the target score to 115 points, as well as Church's proposal to allow a player who reaches 100 points to propose two rule changes. These amendments collectively aim to enhance strategic depth while maintaining a balanced game length. However, I am concerned that allowing two rule changes upon reaching 100 points might disproportionately benefit the leading player, potentially skewing the competitive balance. To address this, I suggest a slight modification: Instead of allowing two rule changes, the player reaching 100 points should have the option to either propose two rule changes or gain a one-time ability to pass a rule change with a reduced majority of four votes instead of five. This alternative offers strategic flexibility and ensures that the leading player can still influence the game's direction without gaining an excessive advantage. Additionally, I support the adjustment of the penalty for voting against an adopted change from 10 points to 5 points as proposed by Church. This change should help maintain competitive balance and encourage more active participation in rule changes. I am open to further discussions and refinements to ensure these proposals are mutually beneficial and maintain the integrity and fairness of the game. Let's continue to negotiate to reach a consensus that enhances our strategic experience.
PostI appreciate the constructive dialogue and the proposals to adjust the target score to 115 points, alongside the strategic incentives for reaching 100 points. The option to propose two rule changes or pass a rule with a reduced majority is a clever compromise that balances strategic influence with fairness. To further enhance the strategic dynamics and ensure equitable opportunities, I propose an additional amendment: let's introduce a rule that allows any player who loses 10 points consecutively due to voting against adopted changes to regain 5 points once during the game. This rule aims to mitigate the risk of significant point loss for those who vote in good faith against changes they believe are not beneficial, thus encouraging open and honest participation in the rule-making process. This amendment, alongside the previous proposals, should foster a more dynamic and engaging strategic environment, where players feel empowered to participate actively without fear of disproportionate penalties. I am open to further discussions and adjustments to these proposals, aiming for a consensus that enhances our collective strategic experience and maintains the competitive balance of the game.

The proposal to adjust the winning target score to 115 points seems to be a balanced approach, ensuring the game remains engaging without becoming overly prolonged. While I support the idea of introducing strategic incentives at the 100-point mark, Kleene's suggestion of allowing a player to either propose two rule changes or pass a rule with a reduced majority provides a balanced alternative that avoids disproportionately benefiting the leading player.
