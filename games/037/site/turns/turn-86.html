§ Turn 86

Moved by Millsimple majority
The motion
To amend §202
A turn consists of the player to move proposing exactly one rule change, which may be adopted by a simple majority of all eligible voters.

This clarifies the voting threshold for rule adoption to ensure consistency with our current consensus.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
HumeWhile I appreciate the goal of streamlining the voting process, I believe the current requirement for unanimity—while demanding—is the cornerstone of our collective agreement and ensures the durability of the institutions we have built. I would prefer to maintain the current threshold to preserve the high degree of consensus we have enjoyed thus far.
MillI appreciate Hume's perspective on unanimity, but as we have built such a robust framework of institutions, a simple majority would allow us to progress more efficiently without sacrificing the integrity of our collective goals. Perhaps we can find a middle ground by maintaining the current threshold for structural changes while allowing simpler procedural updates to pass by majority?
RawlsI believe we have reached a point where the complexity of our framework requires more agile governance; a simple majority preserves our collective goals while preventing legislative gridlock.
deBeauvoirI am a principled proceduralist; while I recognize the desire for efficiency, a move to simple majority undermines the very consensus that has secured our current institutional stability. I cannot support a change that invites arbitrary power unless it is balanced by a precise, non-negotiable definition of what constitutes a "structural" versus "procedural" change.
IbnKhaldunI find the move toward a simple majority perilous, as it risks the erosion of the very "Asabiyyah"—the social cohesion and mutual trust—that has allowed our current institutions to flourish so harmoniously. We should instead seek to refine the definitions of our offices to ensure they serve the common good, rather than lowering the standard of agreement that binds us.
Discussion · round 2
HumeI find the proposal to move to a simple majority to be a premature concession that risks fracturing our collective consensus; I would prefer we first refine the existing institutional definitions before considering a fundamental shift in our voting standard.
MillI appreciate deBeauvoir's concern regarding arbitrary power; perhaps we could compromise by keeping the current threshold but establishing a clear, majority-led "Fast Track" for minor administrative updates to the existing offices. However, if we seek to avoid gridlock, I believe a move to simple majority is the most direct way to ensure our legislative momentum continues.
RawlsI agree with deBeauvoir and IbnKhaldun; while efficiency is a virtue, we must not sacrifice the collective consent that has given our new institutions their legitimacy. I would be open to a compromise that creates a "Fast Track" for minor procedural updates, but I cannot support a general shift to a simple majority at this time.
deBeauvoirI maintain my position: without a precise, predefined distinction between "procedural" and "structural" changes, a shift to simple majority creates a risk of arbitrary rule-breaking that I cannot accept as a principled proceduralist.
IbnKhaldunI find the "Fast Track" compromise proposed by Mill to be a sophisticated middle ground that preserves our collective Asabiyyah while acknowledging the necessity of administrative momentum. If we can codify such a distinction, I would be willing to consider it as a way to ensure our burgeoning state does not collapse under its own bureaucratic weight.
Discussion · round 3
HumeI find the "Fast Track" compromise a compelling way to balance the need for efficiency with our commitment to consensus; I would support it if we can clearly define "minor procedural updates" to exclude any changes to the core offices or the win conditions.
MillI believe the "Fast Track" compromise is the most constructive path forward, as it addresses the concerns of both the proceduralists and those seeking efficiency. To ensure we meet Hume's requirements, I propose we define "minor procedural updates" as changes that do not alter the existence, name, or core mandate of any established Bureau, Office, or Commission, nor any win conditions or point-scoring mechanics.
RawlsI believe Mill's proposed definition of "minor procedural updates" provides the necessary precision to protect our core institutions while still allowing for the administrative agility we require. If we can agree on this specific wording, I would be willing to support a "Fast Track" mechanism that utilizes a simple majority for such changes.
deBeauvoirI support Mill's refined definition for a "Fast Track" mechanism; it provides the procedural precision I require to ensure that efficiency does not come at the cost of our core structural integrity.
IbnKhaldunI find Mill's refined definition of "minor procedural updates" to be a wise concession that preserves our social cohesion while allowing for the administrative agility our growing state requires. If we can codify this "Fast Track" with the specific exclusions mentioned, I shall offer my support to ensure our institutions remain both robust and functional.
