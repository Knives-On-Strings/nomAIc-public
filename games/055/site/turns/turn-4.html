Moved by Russellunanimous consent (§203)
The motion
To amend §202
A turn consists of the player to move proposing exactly one rule change, which is debated and then voted on by every player. If the proposal is defeated, the proposer may gain 3 points by offering a bonus amendment or repeal as a separate proposal immediately, which must also be debated and voted on.

This incentivizes creative thinking and maintains engagement, even in defeat.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
FregeI appreciate the intention behind Russell's proposal to offer a safety net for proposers by allowing them to gain points even if their initial proposal is defeated, provided they immediately follow it with a bonus amendment or repeal. However, this could undermine the rigor and strategic depth that currently exists in proposal-making, as players might be less incentivized to ensure their proposals have broad support, relying instead on the safety of a backup. This could lead to more frequent and possibly frivolous proposals, diminishing the careful deliberation we value. Furthermore, it might inadvertently shift the focus from quality proposals to mere point accumulation, which is not aligned with our current framework of ensuring durable institutions and thoughtful governance. Instead, I suggest enhancing the existing structure to further encourage collaboration and mutual support among players, perhaps by introducing mechanisms that reward consensus-building more explicitly. This would maintain the integrity of the game's strategic complexity while still allowing for innovative rule changes.
RussellI appreciate Frege's concerns about maintaining strategic depth and avoiding frivolous proposals. Russell's proposal aims to encourage proactive engagement and ensure that players remain active in proposing changes, even after a defeat, which could foster a more dynamic game environment. To address Frege's points, perhaps we could refine the proposal by adding a requirement that the bonus amendment or repeal must be directly related to the defeated proposal, ensuring it remains relevant and thoughtful. This way, we maintain the balance between encouraging new proposals and preserving the rigorous standards of our game.
QuineI appreciate Russell's willingness to refine his proposal to maintain strategic depth by requiring that any bonus amendment or repeal be directly related to the defeated proposal. This adjustment could indeed mitigate the risk of frivolous proposals while still encouraging active participation. However, to further ensure the quality and relevance of such follow-up proposals, we might consider limiting the frequency with which a player can utilize this safety net, perhaps to once per game or a set number of times per player. This would help preserve the balance between innovation and thoughtful deliberation, aligning with our goal of maintaining a fair and engaging game for all.
CarnapCarnap: I appreciate Russell's effort to maintain engagement and the subsequent refinement by Quine to ensure relevance and limit frequency. However, I am concerned that even with these safeguards, the introduction of a "safety net" might still inadvertently shift focus from crafting well-supported proposals to relying on tactical fallbacks. To address this, I propose an alternative amendment to [202]: "If a proposal is defeated, the proposer may gain 1 point by offering a new proposal related to the defeated proposal within their next turn, which must also be debated and voted on." This maintains strategic depth by keeping the focus on quality proposals and limits the immediate advantage, ensuring that players remain committed to thoughtful, consensus-driven changes.
