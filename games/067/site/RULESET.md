# The Statute

_As at turn 50._

## Entrenched

**§101.** Every player must, at all times, abide by the ruleset currently in force — including every change adopted since play began.
**§102.** Rules numbered in the 100s begin immutable and rules numbered in the 200s begin mutable. Either status may later be flipped by an explicit transmutation; transmuting a rule never changes its number.
**§103.** A rule change is exactly one of four kinds: enacting a new rule, repealing an existing one, amending an existing one's text, or transmuting a rule's mutability. No other kind of rule change exists.
**§104.** Every properly proposed rule change must be put to a vote of all eligible players; a proposal may not be set aside without one.
**§105.** Every player is an eligible voter, and every eligible voter must cast a vote on every proposal that comes to a vote.
**§106.** A proposal must be written down in the exact words that will be voted on. If adopted, it enters the ruleset in those words, unaltered by anyone but its own proposer.
**§107.** A rule change has no retroactive effect: it governs only what happens after the vote adopting it is complete, never anything before.
**§108.** Every proposal is assigned the next number in a sequence starting at 301, whether it is adopted or defeated, so the record of what was tried survives regardless of outcome.
**§109.** Transmuting a rule's mutability requires the unanimous assent of all eligible voters, and must be proposed and voted on explicitly as a transmutation — never as a side effect of some other change.
**§110.** Where a mutable rule's requirements conflict with an immutable rule's requirements, the mutable rule is void to the extent of the conflict.
**§111.** Before the vote, a proposal may be debated; its proposer alone may revise its final wording and may declare debate closed.
**§112.** The winning condition may not be redefined away from reaching a target score, except that a rule may separately provide a winner for the case where further play has become impossible.
**§113.** Any player may forfeit the game at any time. Forfeiting carries no penalty worse than losing outright.
**§114.** At least one mutable rule must remain in the ruleset at all times, and no rule change may render every future rule change impermissible.
**§115.** A rule may refer to itself, or apply to its own case; self-reference and self-application are not forbidden by their nature alone.
**§116.** Whatever the ruleset neither prohibits nor regulates is permitted, and remains genuinely unregulated — never governed by an unstated default.

## Mutable

**§201.** Players take one fixed turn order, set before play begins. Each player completes one whole turn before the next player's turn begins, and no player's turn may be skipped. Every player's score starts at zero.
**§202.** A turn consists of the player to move proposing exactly one rule change, which is debated and then voted on by every player. If the proposal is adopted, the proposing player throws a six-sided die and gains that many points, unless the proposal was adopted by a simple majority under rule 203, in which case the proposer gains 0 points, or if the proposal was an amendment to rule 204 that reduced the point reward to 0, in which case the proposer gains 0 points regardless of the voting margin. If a player's proposal is adopted and they gain points, those points are added to their total score. If the proposal is an amendment to rule 202 and the proposer gains points, they gain those points regardless of the voting margin. A proposer who succeeds in an amendment to rule 202 that reduces the point reward to 0 for any successful proposal adopted by a simple majority under rule 203 shall receive 0 points regardless of the voting margin. Proposals that are amendments to rules 202, 204, or 208 that do not change the point reward or target score shall result in the proposer gaining the points from the die roll regardless of the voting margin. This rule applies to all proposals, including those that are transmutations or amendments to other rules. Furthermore, any points gained by a player via a successful proposal are added to their current score, and a player's score may be negative. A player's score cannot be lower than -100. If a player's score is -100, any further negative points are discarded. The maximum number of points a player may gain from a single die roll is 6. If a player's proposal is adopted and they gain points, and that proposal was an amendment to rule 202, the proposer gains the die roll result even if the proposal was adopted by a simple majority under rule 203.
**§203.** A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once turn order has carried play through two full circuits of every player, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting.
**§204.** A rule change may be adopted without every voter's assent once the threshold has shifted to a simple majority (rule 203); a player who voted against an adopted change gains 0 points and remains bound by the change exactly as if they had voted for it, unless the change was an amendment to rule 202 that reduced the point reward to 0, in which case the proposer gains 0 points regardless of the voting margin.
**§205.** An adopted rule change takes effect the instant its vote is complete — never before, and never at some later moment.
**§206.** A proposal that fails to be adopted is recorded as defeated. Defeat enacts no rule change: the ruleset is left exactly as it stood before the vote, the proposer's turn ends, and the proposer loses 0 points.
**§207.** Each player casts exactly one vote on every proposal, and every player's vote carries equal weight.
**§208.** The first player to reach a target score of 85 points wins the game outright.
**§209.** The ruleset may never hold more than 100 mutable rules at one time.
**§210.** Players may not discuss the game, or negotiate about it, with one another outside the normal course of play — except that players who are officially teammates may consult freely between themselves, and players may discuss the current ruleset and the implications of proposed rule changes during the debate phase of a turn.
**§211.** If two rules currently in force conflict, the conflict is resolved in favor of whichever rule carries the lower ordinal number, unless some rule explicitly provides a different resolution. However, if a conflict exists between a rule in the 100s and a rule in the 200s or 300s, the rule in the 100s always takes precedence, regardless of the ordinal number.
**§212.** Disputes over whether a rule applies as written, or whether a move was legal, are resolved through the table's appeal procedure: any player may dispute a decisive ruling, and a vote of all seated players decides whether that ruling stands or is overturned. If the vote results in a tie, the ruling stands.
**§213.** If the ruleset is changed so that further play is impossible, or if a move's legality cannot be determined with finality, or if a move appears equally legal and illegal by the best reasoning available and not overruled, then the first player unable to complete a turn wins the game outright. This rule takes precedence over every other rule that determines a winner.
**§311.** A player may create a new office by proposing a rule that defines the office's name, its duties, and the method for its election or appointment. Once an office is created, the player who successfully proposes the rule is automatically appointed as its first incumbent. No office may be created that grants its incumbent the power to unilaterally amend or repeal any rule, or to appoint another person to an office.
