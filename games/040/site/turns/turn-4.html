Moved by Russellsimple majority (§203)
The motion
To amend §211
If two rules currently in force conflict, the conflict is resolved in favor of whichever rule carries the lower ordinal number, unless some rule explicitly provides a different resolution or the proposer of the newer rule wins in the case of a tie.

This amendment ensures a clear resolution in the event of a tie, promoting fairness and strategic rule changes.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
FregeI appreciate the proposal's intent to clarify rule conflicts, but I believe it introduces a bias favoring recent proposers. This could undermine the stability of the ruleset by encouraging frequent, potentially frivolous rule changes to gain an advantage. I propose an amendment instead: "If two rules currently in force conflict, and no explicit resolution is provided, the conflict is resolved by a majority vote among all eligible players." This maintains the importance of consensus and ensures that rule conflicts are handled democratically, preserving the integrity of the ruleset.
RussellI appreciate Frege's emphasis on democratic resolution of conflicts, which aligns well with our game's spirit. However, I'm concerned that frequent voting could slow down gameplay and lead to strategic voting blocs. My proposal aims to strike a balance by ensuring that newer rules aren't automatically disadvantaged, which can encourage thoughtful rule creation and avoid deadlocks in cases of tied ordinal numbers. To address Frege's concerns, how about we incorporate a hybrid approach? We could amend my proposal to include a secondary check: "If two rules currently in force conflict, and the conflict involves tied ordinal numbers, the proposer of the newer rule wins. Otherwise, the conflict is resolved by a majority vote among all eligible players if no explicit resolution is provided." This way, we maintain the democratic process for most conflicts while still recognizing the effort of those proposing new rules. Would this compromise be acceptable to others?
QuineI see the merits in Russell's hybrid approach, which balances the need for democratic resolution with recognition for new rule proposals. However, to further safeguard against potential gaming of the system, I suggest an additional criterion: "If two rules currently in force conflict, and the conflict involves tied ordinal numbers, the proposer of the newer rule wins, provided the newer rule was proposed in the same circuit. Otherwise, the conflict is resolved by a majority vote among all eligible players if no explicit resolution is provided." This ensures that the advantage is not exploited across different circuits, maintaining fairness while still encouraging innovation within the same circuit. What do you think?
CarnapI support Quine's additional criterion, as it effectively prevents exploitation across different circuits while still encouraging innovation within the same circuit. Otherwise, the conflict is resolved by a majority vote among all eligible players if no explicit resolution is provided."
