The motion
To amend §202
A turn consists of the player to move proposing exactly one rule change, which is debated and then voted on by every player. If the proposal is adopted, the proposing player throws a six-sided die and gains that many points, with an additional point if the change increases the ruleset's mutability or decreases the number of immutable rules. Additionally, players earn a bonus point if their proposal successfully amends an existing rule to improve clarity or efficiency, subject to a biannual review. To encourage constructive proposals, players lose 2 points for each rejected proposal.

This amendment incentivizes players to propose beneficial changes while penalizing unconstructive proposals.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
FregeWhile I appreciate the intent to discourage frivolous proposals, the proposed penalty could discourage even constructive amendments due to its punitive nature. Instead, let's consider a system where players lose fewer points for rejected proposals, say 1 point, to balance between discouraging unproductive proposals and encouraging constructive amendments. This approach maintains player engagement and promotes thoughtful rule changes without overly penalizing them for failed attempts.
RussellI appreciate Frege's perspective on maintaining balance. A 1-point penalty is a fair compromise, encouraging constructive amendments without overly discouraging players. To further refine this, perhaps we could introduce a mechanism where, after a certain number of rejections, a player receives guidance or mentorship from a more experienced player. This could enhance learning and ensure that all proposals are well-considered before submission. What do you think?
QuineI appreciate Russell's suggestion for mentorship after repeated rejections, which could indeed foster a more supportive environment. However, to ensure fairness and encourage all players to engage actively, I propose a slight modification: instead of mentorship, let's offer players an option to "buy" a one-time reduction of the rejection penalty by sacrificing a point, if they feel they have a particularly well-considered proposal. This would encourage players to think carefully before using this option while still maintaining an avenue for learning through experience.
CarnapI appreciate Russell's and Quine's suggestions, particularly the mentorship idea, as it aligns with the goal of fostering a supportive learning environment. However, I propose an amendment to Russell's original proposal to balance the penalty system more effectively: instead of a flat 2-point penalty for each rejected proposal, let's implement a progressive penalty system where the first rejected proposal results in a 1-point loss, the second in 2 points, and so on, up to a maximum of 3 points. This encourages players to refine their proposals over time while avoiding excessive discouragement from early setbacks.
WittgensteinWittgenstein: I appreciate the collaborative efforts to refine the proposal. While the progressive penalty system is a thoughtful approach, I suggest we also consider rewarding players who consistently propose successful amendments. For example, a player who successfully amends three rules in a row without rejection could earn a bonus point. This would incentivize thoughtful proposals while balancing the penalty system, fostering a dynamic yet fair environment for all players.
