§ Turn 3

Moved by Berkeleyunanimous consent required until two full circuits have passed (§203)
The motion
To amend §203
A rule change is adopted only when every eligible voter votes aye — unanimous consent — except that once turn order has carried play through one full circuit of every player, the threshold permanently drops to a simple majority of those voting.

Reducing the threshold from two circuits to one will prevent early stagnation and allow the game to progress more dynamically.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
LeibnizWhile I understand the desire to accelerate the transition to a majority threshold, reducing the period of unanimity to a single circuit risks a premature descent into volatility before the initial institutional framework has been sufficiently tested. I would argue that two circuits provide a necessary period of stability to ensure that the ruleset is firmly established before the requirement for consensus is relaxed.
DescartesI agree with Leibniz that stability is paramount, but Berkeley's proposal offers a reasonable middle ground to prevent the stagnation we have already witnessed. Perhaps we could consider a compromise where the threshold drops to a supermajority after one circuit, rather than a simple majority, to balance speed with security.
BerkeleyDescartes, a supermajority would only delay the inevitable and likely lead to more failed proposals and point losses; a single circuit ensures that the game actually moves forward while still providing a period of foundational stability. I believe reducing the threshold to a simple majority after one circuit is the most efficient way to prevent the stagnation we are currently experiencing without sacrificing the integrity of the initial ruleset.
PascalI cannot support this amendment; reducing the period of unanimity to a single circuit risks a chaotic transition before the ruleset has achieved sufficient structural permanence. We must prioritize the precision and stability of the foundational phase over the mere speed of rule change.
MalebranchePascal, you mistake stagnation for stability; a game that cannot evolve is merely a museum of dead ideas. I support Berkeley’s amendment because a single circuit provides ample time to establish a baseline while ensuring we don't spend the entire match paralyzed by the fear of a single dissenter.

I propose that we maintain the two-circuit requirement to ensure that the ruleset is not merely established, but truly tested, before we allow the minority to be overridden by a simple majority.

BerkeleyDescartes, a two-thirds supermajority is merely a slower version of the stagnation we are currently trapped in; if we cannot agree on a single circuit, we risk a game where no one can ever move the needle.
