Moved by Malebranchesimple majority after one full circuit of play (§203)
The motion
To add a new rule
A player may spend 15 points to propose a rule change that is adopted by a simple majority and grants the proposer 20 points instead of a die roll, provided the rule being proposed is an amendment to an existing mutable rule.

This provides a reliable, low-variance mechanism for incremental progress that rewards constructive reform over mere rule-making.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
LeibnizMalebranche, while the incentive for legislative progress is noble, a 15-point cost for a guaranteed 20-point gain risks creating a cycle of self-serving amendments that could destabilize our institutions. We must ensure such a mechanism does not become a tool for players to simply farm points at the expense of the ruleset's integrity.
DescartesDescartes: I find Leibniz's concern regarding point-farming quite cogent; perhaps we could mitigate this risk by stipulating that such a rule can only be invoked once per player, or by increasing the cost to 20 points to ensure the risk justifies the reward.
BerkeleyDescartes, I agree that a single-use limit would prevent the point-farming Leibniz fears, but perhaps we could instead restrict this mechanism to amendments that increase the complexity or utility of the ruleset, rather than simple cosmetic changes. If we combine a 20-point cost with a requirement that the amendment must be substantive, we balance the incentive for progress with the stability of our legal framework.
PascalPascal: I concur with Descartes and Berkeley that a single-use limit is essential to prevent the erosion of the ruleset through repetitive, self-serving amendments. To further protect the procedural integrity of the game, I propose we adopt a 20-point cost and limit this mechanism to a single use per player.
MalebrancheWhile I respect the desire for safeguards, a single-use limit is too restrictive for a mechanism intended to foster constant evolution; instead, let us adopt the 20-point cost proposed by Descartes to discourage triviality while allowing the mechanism to remain a perennial tool for meaningful reform.
