The motion
To add a new rule
Whenever a rule change is adopted without the unanimous consent of all eligible voters, every player who voted in favor of the proposal gains 1 point.

This ensures that supporting a successful proposal is always rewarded, encouraging collaboration and consensus even when unanimity is out of reach.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
NozickRewarding players merely for voting 'aye' on a successful proposal risks corrupting our legislative process, tempting us to vote for bad rules simply to secure a point. We should preserve the integrity of our votes by evaluating proposals on their institutional merit, not an immediate payout.
SenNozick raises a fair concern about vote integrity, but this rule is designed to encourage broad coalitions rather than narrow majorities. For those of us trailing, it provides a modest, steady reward for constructively supporting good legislation that benefits the whole table.
NussbaumI agree with Nozick; this proposal incentivizes forming a permanent, unthinking majority bloc that passes anything just to farm points, which would marginalize dissenting voices like Habermas. We should reward the quality of the proposal itself, not the mere act of joining the winning side.
DworkinNussbaum and Nozick are entirely correct; attaching a material reward to an 'aye' vote fatally undermines the procedural integrity of our legislature. I cannot support a rule that functionally penalizes principled dissent by turning the act of voting into a mere transaction.
HabermasI must side with Nussbaum and Dworkin here: true democratic discourse requires that our assent be driven by the unforced force of the better argument, not a transactional bribe. Let us instead design mechanisms that empower the trailing players without commodifying our consent.
Discussion · round 2

Let's work together to find a formulation that helps trailing players without commodifying consent.
NussbaumI appreciate your flexibility, Sen; a mechanism that rewards players based on their score deficit rather than their voting record is a much healthier way to ensure everyone remains a viable competitor. I look forward to seeing your revised proposal.

Any structural catch-up mechanism must be defined with absolute procedural precision to prevent future scoring ambiguities.
HabermasI urge you to present the exact text of this revision, Sen; a mechanism that directly grants points to the lowest-scoring player whenever a proposal passes would elegantly fulfill our shared goal of structural equity. Let us scrutinize the wording together to ensure it serves the communicative rationality of our game.
Discussion · round 3

SenI propose the following revision: "Whenever a rule change is adopted, the player with the lowest score immediately gains 3 points; if multiple players are tied for the lowest score, each tied player gains 3 points." This directly addresses the structural equity Habermas and Nussbaum championed while preserving the integrity of our votes.
NussbaumThis revised text perfectly captures the structural equity we discussed without compromising our voting integrity, Sen. I am fully in favor of this change, as it ensures players like Habermas remain engaged and competitive.
DworkinThe revised text is procedurally precise and entirely cures the transactional defects of your earlier draft. Although this mechanism will materially benefit our trailing colleagues at my own expense, it applies universally and without ambiguity, so it has my support.
HabermasThis revision brilliantly institutionalizes solidarity, ensuring that our legislative progress structurally uplifts those left behind by the vagaries of the die. I enthusiastically support this text and urge the table to enact it.
