The motion
To add a new rule
Whenever a rule change is adopted, every player who voted aye on that proposal gains 2 points.

This proposal adopts the widely supported revision from Nozick's previous turn, rewarding consensus-building and ensuring those who support successful legislation share in its benefits.

The floor
Discussion · round 1
NozickWhile I appreciate the intent to encourage agreement, rewarding "aye" votes creates a perverse incentive to rubber-stamp proposals simply to farm points. We must preserve the integrity of our voting process by ensuring votes reflect a proposal's merit rather than a guaranteed payout.
SenYou raise a fair point about rubber-stamping, Nozick. I would be happy to amend the proposal to only reward 'aye' votes when a proposal achieves unanimous consent, ensuring we only incentivize genuine consensus rather than blind agreement.
NussbaumSen, requiring unanimous consent means every player would receive the 2 points simultaneously, which doesn't change our relative standings but simply accelerates Dworkin toward the 100-point finish line. To avoid both rubber-stamping and uniform point inflation, we should perhaps focus on rewarding the author of a good proposal rather than the voters.
DworkinI agree with Nussbaum's structural analysis, but my objection is more fundamental: tying points to the act of voting aye corrupts the integrity of the ballot. We must vote on the merits of the rule change, not for a guaranteed payout, so I cannot support this proposal in any form.
HabermasI must echo Nussbaum's astute observation: uniform score inflation simply hands Dworkin a swifter victory while corrupting our deliberative process. If we want to foster genuine consensus, we should instead craft mechanisms that reward the author of a successful proposal, ideally in a way that helps trailing players close the gap.
Discussion · round 2
NozickI agree entirely with Nussbaum and Habermas; amending this to require unanimous consent merely inflates all scores equally, which hands a massive advantage to the frontrunner, Dworkin. Since the core mechanism of rewarding votes is inherently flawed, I urge that this proposal be defeated so we can focus on better structural reforms.
SenI hear the consensus loud and clear: rewarding votes is structurally flawed and disproportionately helps the leader. I will amend the proposal to instead grant 4 points to the author of an adopted rule change, provided their score is below 50, which directly assists those of us furthest behind without corrupting the ballot.
NussbaumSen, your amendment elegantly resolves the vote-buying issue while offering a targeted catch-up mechanism for our trailing players. I fully support this revision, as it incentivizes constructive play from those who need the points most without handing an unearned advantage to the leader.
DworkinWhile Sen's amendment removes the perverse incentive to buy votes, setting an arbitrary threshold of 50 points is structurally rigid and inelegant. We already have a dynamic mechanism for trailing players under Rule 312, making this new proposal redundant clutter.
HabermasSen's proposed amendment offers a much-needed structural boost for those of us struggling at the bottom, and unlike Rule 312, it provides a fixed safety net rather than a moving target. I will gladly support this revised proposal to help balance the scales and foster a more competitive game.
Discussion · round 3
NozickI agree with Dworkin that a static 50-point threshold is arbitrary and poorly designed, as it excludes active competitors like Nussbaum and myself while duplicating the dynamic catch-up mechanism we already have in Rule 312. I urge everyone to vote against this proposal so we can pursue more equitable reforms.
SenWith clear support from Nussbaum and Habermas, I hereby amend the proposal to read: "Whenever a rule change is adopted, if the proposing player's score is strictly below 50, that player gains 4 points." and I declare debate closed.
HabermasSen has wisely closed debate on a targeted reform that prevents the game from devolving into an early coronation for Dworkin. I urge Nussbaum to hold firm in her support; empowering the lower tier ensures the game remains competitive long enough for her to overtake the leaders.
